Controlled Variation

Controlled Variation

  • Economists often rely on “natural experiments”
    • Nuclear power plant closure
    • Buffer implementation
    • Sustainable, bamboo gloves vs. less expensive, non-socially responsible gloves


  • A big problem is that a lot of the wanted data is unobserved, uncontrolled, and unaccounted for.

Controlled Variation

  • Lab provides a medium for the researcher to directly vary the variable of interest \(X_1\), while controlling for much more of \(\tilde{X}\) than with organic empirical data

Lab vs. Field?

Commonly cited characteristics

Labs:

  • Tighter control over subjects
  • More flexibility
  • More accessible

Field:

  • More “realistic”
  • “Students do not represent farmers”
  • Implied greater relevance to policy makers

The true goal

The causal effect of \(X_1\) on \(Y\), holding fixed \(\tilde{X}\).

  • Labs allow researchers to fix a lot of \(\tilde{X}\) and control for other.

  • Field experiments might have different values \(\tilde{X}'\)

  • “Reality” might have even different values \(\tilde{X}''\). Which is a better approximation, \(\tilde{X}\) or \(\tilde{X}'\)?

  • Example: Japanese fishermen

Use whichever one benefits you the most

Labs:

  • Tighter control over \(\tilde{X}\)
  • Useful for game theory/behavioral assumptions

Field:

  • Wider and less controlled \(\tilde{X}\)
  • Better implications for specific policies or decisions that effect diverse groups of people

Other Critiques

Students are not representitive

  • Irrelevant for theory as it’s independent of participant pools
  • For specific behavior, might be beneficial to diversify subject pool

Stakes are trivial

  • Well, what should the right level of stakes be?
  • Can always just vary the stakes (Controlled Variation at work!)

Too small sample size

  • Modern experiments are run with 100+ or even 1000+ subjects
  • Techniques, such as Permutation Testing (Heckman et. al 09), can be used to analyzed small sample and/or skewed data

Participants are inexperienced

  • Can potentially be controlled for
  • Can even be analyzed (learning effects)

Hawthorne effects

  • In this day in age, we all know we’re being observed by someone/some corporation

Self selection

  • Can control for subject background in Lab
  • Field might have potential for non-compliance

Comparison with surveys

  • Experiments strongly complement surveys
  • Surveys are much easier to generate, but only solicit stated preference
  • Experiments can validate survey results as subjects act via incentive

Final thoughts

  • Experimental work, both lab and field, provide an additional angle to control variation
  • Empirical methodologies and data are compliments, not substitutes
  • All trying to solve the same problem of generalizability
